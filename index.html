Hursley Living Lab

Interactive webpage to display data about the rooms within Hursley House.

The aim of this project was to use Internet of Things technologies and IBM products to improve the experience of visiting or working at the Hursley Site and to monitor the rooms within Hursley House, IBM's Executive Briefing Centre.

The first stage of the project started with the Executive Briefing Centre in Hursley House, which is a grade 2* listed building for which there is a rigorous approval process to make any changes. This was part of the drive to use IoT technologies, which can work around these restrictions. One of the major issues with Hursley House is that it has a Victorian heating system so the rooms are often too cold or too hot.

My role was to design an interactive web page. to represent the data being collected by the sensors, so the briefing centre staff could monitor the status of the rooms, and clients could see IBM and partner technologies working together to achieve an innovative solution.

I then created a Brand and Style Guide to give to the front-end developers on the team so that we could work together to execute the designs to the highest standard. The guide specified a colour palette, typography, contained all visual assets and dictated how the page shoud respond to different displays.

Nike Revive

A branded food heating device for mountain bikers.

The project brief was to design a Nike branded food heating device specifically for mountain bikers so they could refuel with a hot meal whilst on a ride. I started by deconstructing the brand. I looked at Nike's values, who their customers are and aspire to be, how the brand is perceived in the media and the materials and shapes commonly used in their products.

I then looked into popular mountain biking brands and the experiences that people have when enjoying the sport. I also conducted extensive user research into people who enjoy mountain biking by conducting interviews and online surveys and reading articles and blogs , during which I gained insight into why people go mountain biking, what they typically take with them, how long they ride for and where they like to go.

The evidence of all the research conducted prior to and during the design can be found here.

I created a User Experience Scenario which is not an explanation of how the persona uses the product, but what they experience when they do. The scenario is based on the research I conducted regarding how Nike customers typically feel and want to feel when interacting with their products. For example, Nike products are often perceived to be fashionable, foreword-thinking, high quality, powerful and victorious.

The scenario depicts my persona setting out on a difficult and ambitious ride. He becomes tired and starts to struggle so he gets the Nike Revive device out of his backpack and enjoys a hot nutritious meal in minutes. Now he has the necessary energy to finish his ride and feels victorious for doing so.

I went through many iterations of designs, made sketch models and tested them on people for size and ease of use. The divide uses an exothermic reaction to heat the food. The materials I chose (HDPE and Silicone) had to be able to withstand high temperatures from the reaction, as well as survive any impacts that might occur on a ride.

I designed a mechanism so water can be stored in the lid of the device and then released by sliding a switch, so the water falls down the central cylinder and reacts with the capsule at the bottom. Before their ride, the mountain biker loads the stainless steel vacuum flask with a pre-cooked meal of their choice, fills the cavity in the lid with water and then stores it in their backpack or car. When desired the user can activate the device and their food will heat up in minutes. At the end of use the capsule is unscrewed and discarded and can be replaced ready for next time.

E.on Energy Save

An app to encourage families to save energy in the home by using gamification principles.

The brief was to create an app for E.on to encourage families to save energy in the home. I started by researching and testing apps that use gamification principles and how they influenced behaviour change. I decided initially to use positive reinforcement in the form of incentives and rewards as well as competition.


My initial concept was to allow families to compete with their neighbours or other families in their area for the lowest energy usage per person and the most improved energy reduction, and gain rewards such as family days out for doing so. This was to involve the children of the family as they could compete with their friends. I found that when people know they are being compared to others they are more likely to want to look good and therefore be more conscious of their energy use.

When I presented my app concept to my original focus group there were some elements that they really liked which were having the ability to monitor their energy use over time and both the adults and children liked the rewards system. However the didn’t like the concept of competing with their neighbours as they thought it was too personal and an invasion of their privacy.

Instead they said that an app that stressed more on educating the family (especially the children) on ways to save energy e.g. turning lights off would be more beneficial to them.

Based on the focus group feedback I made some significant changes to the design. It is now more focussed on educating and rewarding the family for saving energy and less on competing with others- this makes a more family friendly yet still fun concept. The data page now contains more details on the household’s energy use in a way that is simple to understand. The family can access an individual house-member’s usage so become competitive between users.

The quick-access screensaver allows the family to see instantly whether they are above or below their tariff level. If the globe is green then their “E.ons score” is above their tariff (saving energy) and if it’s red they are using more than they should be. The user page allows parents to monitor their children’s energy use more closely and make them more aware of the impact they have on the household. It can also be used to switch off lights and appliances remotely.



I created an experience prototype video to show how the app fits into family life.



Crewmaster

A sailing racecourse plotter and tracker.

This project is an exercise in experimenting with form and human interaction with an interface. I created a "Form and User Board" which inspired my sketches and helped me understand the demographic and ethnography of sailors. I wanted to use complex curvature in my design to evoke the movement of the sea.

Whilst developing my design I also had to consider how it would be attached to the boat when the sailor couldn't hold it, and realised that too much complex curvature would mean it many not be possible to do so securely. Therefore I went for a more subtle curvature by using undercuts and changes in material and texture.

I created a series of sketch models from card models to foam models to experiment with the form and proportions before I started on the final high fidelity model. To make the final model I cut out the general shape with a band saw and a hack saw, then used a combination of belt sanders and hand tools to refine it. I built up layers of acrylic around two sides so that I could slide the perspex screen underneath and cut a hole for the button.

I then sprayed the whole model with filler primer, let it dry, sanded it down the repeated several times. I masked off certain areas so I could achieve the two-tone effect with car spray paint, then added a logo using waterslide paper once it had dried fully. I created the screen display in Photoshop and then slotted it between the model and the clear perspex to create the final visual.



IBM Integration Bus

An animated video to promote IBM Integration Bus on Cloud.





This video was released alongside the general availability of the product and is hosted on the IBM Integration Youtube channel here. We started by deconstructing the script (written by marketing) and split it into multiple scenes, before sketching out storyboards for each scene and how we could move between them. The scenes were then split up between myself and my two fellow interns and we began to make the assets on Illustrator, and then animated them in After Effects.


Once each scene was roughly animated we added the voiceover to get the timings and scene changes exact. We went through multiple iterations of the script whilst working on the animations so had to be very flexible, and scenes were cut and redrafted as we went along. Finally I stitched all the scenes together in Premier Pro and added sound effects and scene transitions.


KOKA

A set of products for boiling, steaming and draining vegetables.

The brief set by Dunelm was to design a product to enhance and improve cooking in the home, with sustainability at the centre of the design. I decided to scope the problem to people who live in shared accommodation where space is limited. I observed people in shared housing trying to cook over several evenings and found that one of the pain points for them was limited hob space, as if they were making a meal they might need several pans for different vegetables.



To solve the problem I designed a veg drainer inside which the vegetables are boiled within a pan, so many different foods can be boiled in one pan. It also allows for both boiling and steaming of veg depending on how much water is put into the pan. To drain the veg the drainer is simply lifted out of the pan and the water falls through the holes in the bottom. This means that the water can be used by another person using the kitchen and there is no need for them to re-boil any water. Alternatively several people can use one large pan at one time and remove their veg whenever it is ready.

The main body of the drainer is made from Nylon as it can be injection moulded and therefore produced relatively cheaply, and can also withstand very high temperatures. The grip is made from Silicone as it does not conduct heat and is malleable so will be comfortable for the user to hold.

Scott Safety

A weeklong project to design a quick release carry case.

The brief was to Design a new carry case to hold an emergency breathing device. It must be strapped to the body and allow the mouthpiece to be donned in 30 seconds. I started by determining the smallest footprint that would still fit the device inside securely and explored different possible orientations on the body.

When testing out different orientations on the body I found that the middle option would be the most feasible as when the catch is opened from the front the product falls forward, allowing it to be removed from the case and donned quickly.

I made models to illustrate the different options for the opening position and went through several iterations until I was happy with the way the product was presented when the case was opened.
